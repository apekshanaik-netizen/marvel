## Working Principle

The MQ135 works based on chemical adsorption and semiconductor physics:

| In Clean Air:

1.Oxygen molecules in the air are adsorbed onto the SnO₂ surface.

2.These oxygen molecules capture electrons from the sensor, which increases the sensor’s electrical resistance.

3.The microcontroller sees a high voltage across the load resistor in this condition.

| In Presence of Harmful Gases:

1.Gases such as CO₂, NH₃, NOx, or smoke react with the adsorbed oxygen on the SnO₂ surface.

2.This reaction releases electrons back to the SnO₂ layer, reducing its resistance.

3.The voltage across the load resistor decreases, indicating pollutant presence.

## Calibration

### Why Calibration is Important

1.Baseline Resistance (R₀):
Every MQ135 sensor has a baseline resistance
𝑅<sub>o</sub> in clean air. This value is used as a reference to calculate gas concentrations.

2.Accuracy:
Without calibration, the sensor may underestimate or overestimate gas concentrations, leading to false alarms or missed detections.

3.Repeatability:
Proper calibration ensures consistent readings across multiple measurements or over time.

## Calibration process

The MQ135 gas sensor is a small but powerful device that helps us measure the quality of the air around us. It can detect gases like carbon dioxide, ammonia, nitrogen oxides, smoke, and other harmful pollutants. When it senses these gases, its internal tin dioxide layer changes its electrical resistance. However, the raw readings it gives are not automatically meaningful. Just seeing a voltage number isn’t enough to know how polluted the air is. That’s where calibration comes in—it’s the process that allows the sensor to translate its signals into accurate, understandable gas concentrations.

Before the sensor can give reliable readings, it needs to warm up. Inside the MQ135, there’s a tiny heater that keeps the sensing layer at the right temperature so that it reacts properly with gases. For a brand-new sensor, this warm-up can take a day or two. During this time, the sensor stabilizes, and we shouldn’t rely on the readings just yet.

Once it’s warmed up, the next step is to establish a baseline resistance in clean air. This is essentially figuring out what the sensor “looks like” when the air is safe and pollutant-free. By measuring its resistance under these conditions, we can calculate a reference value called
𝑅<sub>o</sub>. This baseline is critical because all future readings will be compared to it. If the air around the sensor contains harmful gases, the sensor’s resistance will drop, and by comparing it to 𝑅<sub>o</sub> , we can figure out how much gas is present.

In short, calibration transforms the MQ135 from a simple electronic component into a sensor that can understand the air we breathe. It gives the sensor a “baseline memory” of clean air, so any deviation tells us immediately when something in the environment is unsafe. With proper calibration, this tiny device becomes a key player in creating healthier, safer spaces for people to live and work.
